Transformer-Based Deep Neural Network Design for Gravitational Lensing Substructure ClassificationThe search for strong gravitational lenses and the characterization of dark matter substructures have undergone a major paradigm shift. Traditionally, automated detection relied on Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) due to their built-in inductive biases, such as locality and translation equivariance, which excel at identifying localized features like arcs and Einstein rings. However, the astronomical signatures of gravitational lensing—ranging from massive galaxy-cluster scale distortions to minuscule dark matter subhalos—span global, non-local spatial structures that pose a challenge for standard CNN backbones.Recent advancements have introduced Vision Transformers (ViTs) and attention-based architectures to this domain. By leveraging multi-head self-attention mechanisms, transformers model global relationships across an entire image starting from the very first layer, capturing long-range dependencies and subtle morphological anomalies with high fidelity. This report provides an exhaustive, expert-level architectural guide for implementing a transformer-based Deep Neural Network (DNN) designed for strong gravitational lensing classification, with a specific focus on identifying dark matter substructures (such as Cold Dark Matter subhalos, axion vortex structures, or smooth, featureless halos) within simulated and real scientific imaging.Macro-Architectural Design of the Transformer NetworkDesigning an attention-based classifier for gravitational lensing requires balancing the high representational capacity of transformers with the data-starved regimes typical of astrophysics. Because vanilla transformers lack spatial inductive biases, choosing the right macro-architecture is critical for successful convergence. An input image is divided into a sequence of non-overlapping patches, flattened, and projected into a latent vector space. Learnable 1D position embeddings are added to these vectors to preserve spatial coordinates before feeding them into the transformer encoder. Each encoder block applies Multi-Head Self-Attention (MHSA) followed by an MLP block with residual connections and layer normalization. This architecture is highly effective when paired with transfer learning from massive datasets like ImageNet-21k, allowing the network to leverage general features and adapt them to astrophysical classification.Shifted Window (Swin) TransformerSwin Transformers address the high computational complexity of global attention by computing self-attention within local, non-overlapping windows. By shifting the window partition between consecutive layers, the model enables cross-window connections, generating hierarchical representations at multiple resolutions. This structural bias behaves similarly to a CNN's pooling mechanism, making Swin Transformers significantly more data-efficient and high-performing on cosmological mass mapping tasks than vanilla ViTs under restricted training sets.Physics-Informed Hybrid Architectures (LensPINN)A hybrid design, such as LensPINN, integrates physical constraints directly into the neural network's forward pass. By combining a CNN framework with a ViT encoder, the network can ingest physical parameters derived from the gravitational lensing equation. The deflection angles, governed by the mass distribution of the lens, are learned or constrained by the transformer encoder, while background light profiles are processed by the CNN. This integration significantly reduces the search space during training, improving generalization to unseen dark matter substructures.Image Preprocessing Pipelines for Astronomical DataData collected from astronomical surveys like the Legacy Survey of Space and Time (LSST), the Hubble Space Telescope (HST), or Euclid contain distinct instrument signatures, high noise levels, and low-contrast target structures. A highly specialized preprocessing pipeline is essential to enhance physical lensing features before tokenization.The preprocessing sequence begins with raw FITS image parsing, followed by pixel stretching to compress the dynamic range, noise injection to simulate instrument response, and geometric augmentations to enforce rotational invariance.Clipping and Mathematical StretchingStrong lensing arcs and Einstein rings are often masked by the high luminosity of the foreground deflector galaxy. To reveal these faint background structures, the dynamic range of the image must be compressed. This is achieved by first clipping negative pixels (arising from background subtraction noise) to zero and then applying a mathematical scale transformation. A square-root stretch is typically formulated as:$$I_{\text{stretched}} = \sqrt{\max(0, I_{\text{raw}})}$$[cite: 1]Alternatively, an asinh stretch is applied to preserve linear relationships in low-flux regions while compressing high-flux peaks:$$I_{\text{stretched}} = \operatorname{asinh}\left(\frac{I_{\text{raw}}}{\beta}\right)$$where $\beta$ is a noise scaling parameter.Observational Noise ModelingTo bridge the gap between idealized simulations and real sky observations, realistic noise profiles must be injected during training. This involves simulating both detector read noise and photon shot noise.Poissonian Noise: Models the statistical fluctuations of incoming photons, particularly dominant in high-flux areas (such as the bright cores of deflector galaxies).Gaussian Noise: Simulates the background electronic read noise of the imaging detector.The preprocessing pipeline must adjust these parameters to match the targeted instrument specifications, aiming for a consistent Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) of approximately 25.Coordinate and Dihedral AugmentationsBecause gravitational lenses are physically isotropic, the model must exhibit rotational invariance. To enforce this without expanding model parameters, the dataset is augmented using the $D_4$ dihedral group (eight combinations of $90^\circ$ rotations and horizontal/vertical flips). Additionally, random pixel shifts within a range of $[-5, +5]$ pixels are applied to prevent the model from overfitting to the center of the image, simulating telescope pointing variations.Image Tokenization Strategies for Gravitational Lensing ImagesTokenization is the process of translating a continuous 2D image into a discrete sequence of 1D vector embeddings that can be ingested by the self-attention layers. These flattened patches are mapped to the model dimension $D$ using a trainable linear projection matrix $E \in \mathbb{R}^{(P^2 \cdot C) \times D}$. To preserve spatial context, a learnable position embedding $E_{\text{pos}} \in \mathbb{R}^{(N + 1) \times D}$ is added to the projected patch representations:$$z_0 = [x_{\text{class}}; \, x_p^1 E; \, x_p^2 E; \, \dots; \, x_p^N E] + E_{\text{pos}}$$[cite: 4]where $x_{\text{class}}$ represents a prependable classification token.Self-Supervised Pretraining and Unified Modeling FrameworksDue to the limited availability of confirmed real-world gravitational lenses, training large vision transformers from scratch is prone to overfitting. To mitigate this, self-supervised learning (SSL) techniques are used to pretrain the network on large synthetic catalogs before fine-tuning on specific observational classes.Masked Autoencoder (MAE) PretrainingMasked Autoencoders are highly effective for learning representation structures in strong gravitational lensing datasets. The MAE pipeline operates as a self-supervised reconstruction task: during pretraining, a high masking ratio (typically $75\%$) is applied to the image patches. The ViT encoder only processes the remaining $25\%$ visible, non-masked patches, significantly reducing memory and computational overhead during training. The output representations of the encoder, combined with learnable masked tokens, are passed to a lightweight, asymmetric transformer decoder designed to reconstruct the original pixel values of the masked patches.This reconstruction task forces the ViT encoder to learn the underlying physics of light deflection, background source distributions, and instrumental noise models. Once pretrained, the decoder is discarded, and the encoder's weights are fine-tuned for the downstream classification task.Contrastive Learning (SimCLR / DINO)Contrastive learning techniques, such as SimCLR, optimize the representation space by maximizing similarity between different augmented views of the same lensed image while minimizing similarity between views of different images. Two stochastic augmentations (such as random rotations and translations) are applied to an input image to create a positive pair. The encoder maps these images to a lower-dimensional latent bottleneck, and a projection head projects them into a space where the Normalized Temperature-backed Cross-Entropy (NT-Xent) loss is computed:$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{NT-Xent}} = -\log \frac{\exp(\operatorname{sim}(z_i, z_j)/\tau)}{\sum_{k=1}^{2N} \mathbb{1}_{[k \neq i]} \exp(\operatorname{sim}(z_i, z_k)/\tau)}$$[cite: 9]where $\operatorname{sim}(u, v) = \frac{u^T v}{\Vert{}u\Vert{} \Vert{}v\Vert{}}$ represents cosine similarity, $\tau$ is a temperature parameter, and $N$ is the batch size. This teaches the transformer to focus on invariant physical structures (like the macro-geometry of the lens) while ignoring high-frequency noise.Classification Head and Decoding MechanismsOnce patch tokens have been processed by the transformer encoder blocks, the resulting high-dimensional representations must be decoded into classification probabilities.CLS Token DecodingThe standard ViT decoding technique utilizes a prepended, learnable [CLS] token. This token interacts with all patch tokens via self-attention across all encoder layers. The final state of the [CLS] token, representing an aggregate global representation of the image, is passed to an MLP classification head:$$\hat{y} = \operatorname{Softmax}(\operatorname{MLP}(z_L^0))$$[cite: 4]where $z_L^0$ is the output of the first element (the CLS token position) from the final ($L$-th) transformer layer.Global Average Pooling (GAP)For highly symmetric and noisy cosmological structures, global average pooling over all output patch tokens can outperform [CLS] token decoding. The spatial patch embeddings are averaged along the sequence dimension:$$\bar{z}_L = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N z_L^i$$This averaged representation is then passed directly to a single-layer linear classifier. GAP acts as a strong regularizer, reducing overfitting on local high-frequency noise artifacts.Multi-Task Decoders for Parameter ConstrainingA powerful configuration involves combining the classification head with auxiliary regression or reconstruction heads. For instance, a joint classifier-decoder network can simultaneously classify the dark matter substructure model and reconstruct the unlensed source galaxy profile or predict specific physical parameters (such as the Einstein radius $\theta_E$ or lens center coordinate $\theta_c$). The global network is trained on a joint loss function:$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} = \lambda_{\text{classification}} \mathcal{L}_{\text{BCE}} + \lambda_{\text{reconstruction}} \mathcal{L}_{\text{MSE}}$$[cite: 9]where $\mathcal{L}_{\text{BCE}}$ represents Binary (or Categorical) Cross-Entropy for substructure classification, $\mathcal{L}_{\text{MSE}}$ represents Mean Squared Error for the auxiliary task, and the $\lambda$ coefficients balance the loss contributions.Synthesis of Recommended Production ConfigurationsFor researchers implementing a classifier on gravitational
